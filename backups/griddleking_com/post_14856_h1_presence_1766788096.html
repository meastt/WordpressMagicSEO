Grapeseed Oil Smoke Point: The Impact on Cast Iron Cooking Let’s start with the basics. You must have heard of the term “Smoke Point” by now. It’s essentially the temperature at which cooking oils start to break down and smoke. This can happen due to the thermal breakdown of fat species, moisture content and impurities present in the oil. When this smoke point is reached, the oil starts to oxidize and break down due to heat, and no matter how tasty the food looks, it will have a bitter taste and an unpleasant smell. In other words, with regard to cooking, the smoke point of the oil you use should be taken very seriously. The main purpose of using an oil in cooking is to add flavor and prevent food from sticking to the cooking surface. But, if you use an oil with an inappropriately too high of a smoke point for that particular cooking method, it will simply end in a disaster. And since cast iron cookware is basically a form of radiant heat transfer, the oil's smoke point is even more critical, as it will be exposed to higher temperatures. Speaking of which, when it comes to cooking with a cast iron, the most commonly used oil is grapeseed oil due to its light taste and smoke point of about 392-410 degrees Fahrenheit. For reference, the smoke points of some other oils - Peanut oil is 446–470°F, Sunflower oil is 450°F and Canola oil is just 400°F. So, while grapeseed oil has a slightly lower smoke point than the others, it’s not too low to be a major issue, when used with cast iron cookware. Additionally, the fatty acid profile of grapeseed oil is also something to consider, as it can help us determine the stability of the oil and hence, the smoke point. Grapeseed oil is very high in monosaturated fats, which are believed to help reduce cholesterol, preserve heart health and provide antioxidants. It is also high in linoleic acid, which makes it a very healthy oil for cooking. It is also much less likely to go rancid, because it is virtually free from trans-fats, meaning it won’t break down as quickly when exposed to heat. Furthermore, its light taste doesn’t really overpower the flavor of food. It also doesn’t contain any artificial flavors, colors or preservatives, which makes it a healthy oil to use for all your cast-iron cooking ventures. To sum it up, the smoke point of grapeseed oil is ideal for cast iron cooking, and its healthy fatty acid profile and light flavor make it the perfect all-round oil for all your culinary endeavors, whether it’s a skillet-cooked meal or a cake from the oven. Understanding the Smoke Point of Cooking Oils Now that you have a better understanding of how infrared heating and the smoke point impact cast iron cooking, let’s further understand the importance of smoke point and how it relates to the type of oil you choose for cooking. First of all, it’s essential to know which oils you should use for raw applications versus cooked ones. For example, delicate oils such as extra virgin olive oil are better suited for raw recipes, as they tend to have a low smoke point. On the other hand, you can use vegetable oil and other higher smoke point oils for cooking. Moreover, you must also be aware when to add oil to a pan! Too little won’t provide enough moisture, while adding too much will coat the food too heavily and cause smoking or burning. Lastly, never assume that all oils perform similarly with different cooking methods. The smoke points of different oils can vary drastically, so you must experiment and find out which one is best for you. Moving on to why people usually prefer grapeseed oil for cast-iron cooking - the number one reason is the smoke point. As we already discussed, its smoke point is about 392-410 degrees Fahrenheit, which makes it a great option for high-temperature cooking since cast iron cookware is usually heated to about 350-400 degrees Fahrenheit. Why Grapeseed Oil is a Good Option for Cast Iron Cooking Now that you’ve gained a better understanding of how smoke point affects the flavor and quality of your food and why grapeseed oil is the best oil for cast iron cooking, let’s unravel why grapeseed oil should be your top choice for your culinary needs. One of the best things about grapeseed oil is its light taste, which means it won’t overpower your recipes like some other heavier oils. It also has minimal monounsaturated fat content, which is perfect for cooking over high heat. Since it contains no cholesterol, it will not add any unhealthy fats to your food. Moreover, its high smoke point ensures that your food won't get burned or overcooked, even when you're dealing with delicacy or complex recipes. Lastly, it's much less likely to go rancid than other cooking oils, which is another factor that makes it an excellent oil for high-heat cooking. How Smoke Point Affects the Flavor and Quality of Your Food Now that you understand why it’s important to pay attention to smoke point when cooking with cast iron, let’s dig a bit deeper into how it affects the flavor and quality of your food. The smoke point of your cooking oil impacts the flavor and quality of your food in many ways. For starters, it affects the texture of your food. If you use oil with a low smoke point, then you’ll end up with a greasy, sticky texture, which isn’t very pleasant. Furthermore, when high-temperature cooking occurs with an oil that has a too low of a smoke point, you end up with an unpleasant, bitter taste in your food. Unfortunately, with that kind of taste, no amount of seasoning can salvage your dish. The smoke point of oil also impacts its nutritional value. For instance, when you cook at high temperatures with an oil that has a low smoke point, it breaks down into free radicals, which can be harmful for your health. On the other hand, if you use an oil with a higher smoke point, you can be assured that your food is safe from free radical damage. So, to sum it up, the higher the smoke point of an oil you use for cooking, the better the flavor, texture, and nutrition of your food will be. A Few Tips and Tricks for Better Cast Iron Cooking Now that you’ve gone over the smoke points related to cast iron cooking, let me provide you with some helpful tips and tricks to ensure that your cooking comes out flawless every time. 1. Preheat the pan properly: Preheating your pan is a must when cooking with cast iron. To preheat the pan, heat it over medium-high heat for 4-5 minutes before adding oil or cooking food. Doing so will help prevent sticking, and also ensure a consistent cooking temperature. 2. Oil it properly: If you’re using a very thin oil, like canola oil, infuse it with herbs, spices and citrus for extra flavor. On the other hand, if you’re using thicker oils, like coconut oil and grapeseed oil, coating the pan with a thin, even layer is enough to prevent sticking. 3. Clean your pan properly: To clean your cast-iron pan, never use soap, as it will remove the protective layer of oil on the pan and may cause sticking in the future. Just wipe the pan with a cloth and warm water and, if needed, use a little cooking oil to remove any stubborn bits. 4. Store it correctly : To maintain the longevity of your pan, never store it huddled in a drawer. Instead, store it on a low shelf with a few pieces of paper in between, so that it won’t come into contact with other pieces of cookware.
